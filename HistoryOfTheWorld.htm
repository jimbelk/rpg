History of the World

This is a short history of the campaign world.  The written history of this world dates back to the destruction of the thoughtstone by an extraplanar party of githyanki, an event known as “the Awakening”.  Before this time, virtually all sentient creatures on this plane were under the direct mental control of the illithids.

Dates in this world are counted in years since the Awakening (written xxxx P.A.), where one year represents about the same amount of time that it does on your world.  The current year is 461 P.A.

1. Ancient History

Very little is known about the history of the world before the Awakening.  It is known that, in the preceding century, a series of cataclysmic events left most of the cities of the world in ruins, including an apparent extraplanar invasion by a horde of fiends (both demons and devils). Very few written records survive from that time, though many of the largest dungeons of the world date back to that period.

The ruins on the surface are much older (by several thousand years), and clearly indicate the existence of an ancient surface civilization. The nature of this civilization is a complete mystery, as is its eventual fate. Some sages have speculated that this may have been the original home plane of the githyanki and githzerai, before the arrival of the illithids, while others have argued that the surface was populated by some unknown progenitor race, perhaps a common ancestor of xephs, duergar, urdinnir, drow, and svirfneblin.

2. Extinct Races

Two extinct races play an important role in the history of the world: the xephs and the urdunnir.

The xephs (or humans) were a tall and skinny race, about a foot taller than a typical dwarf. Their skin was smooth and light brown, and they only rarely wore facial hair. Xephs were among the most flexible of all races, with wizards and clerics as powerful as the greatest elf, and fighters and psychic warriors as strong as the best dwarf.

The urdunnir were an offshoot of the dwarven race. In addition to the usual beards, they had grey hair on top of their heads, and silvery eyes.  The urdinnir were not as cruel as their duergar cousins, though they shared the same love of mining and craftsmanship. The urdinnir did not possess psionic powers, and some scholars believe that the illithids bred the duergar from urdinnir stock sometime before the Awakening.

3. Awakening War (1–97 P.A.)

Immediately after the Awakening, the “grand alliance” of xephs, duergar, urdinnir, drow, and svirfneblin fought fiercely against the illithids, destroying all of their cities in the Middleworld as well as many of their great machines. This alliance was fragile, however, as the duergar and drow were greedy to claim territory for themselves, while urdinnir and svirfneblin were distrustful of other races.

The xephs were natural leaders of the alliance, for they had a talent for diplomacy, and managed to rally all of the other races to their cause. This proved disastrous for them, for it made the elimination of the xephs the foremost goal of the illithids. In 23 P.A., the illithids unleashed the Purge, a storm of psionic energy that spread through the world, completely eradicating the xephs as well as several minor races.

Following the loss of the xephs, the alliance fractured, with each race making war on the illithids separately.  Soon conflicts over captured territory (as well as the former territory of the xephs) led to open warfare between the duergar and the drow.

This led to a period (roughly 23 P.A. – 97 P.A.) of intermittent warfare between all the major races.  The duergar and urdinnir continued their fight against the illithids, but also had to contend with the drow, who seemed determined to claim the Middleworld for themselves.  During this time, the svirfneblin began to lose interest in war, and hid themselves in increasingly fortified cities.

4. Far Realm Invasion (97–139 P.A.)

In 97 P.A., the illithids opened a portal to a distant corner of the Far Realm, an extraplanar space of pure madness. Hoping to summon allies, they instead awoke a primordial alien intelligence known as Y'chak (the Violet Flame).

This was not entirely a coincidence: it was later found that the illithid portal project had been subtly influenced by the aboleths, an ancient race living in the deepest oceans of the Lowerworld. Apparently the aboleths worship a collection of beings that they refer to as the “Elder Evils”, of which Y'chak is one.

For some unfathomable reason, Y'chak began to send horror after horror to this world, including a large number of kaorti, grell, cloakers, gibbering mouthers, and so forth. The laws of reality seemed to bend wherever the newcomers would arrive, and great swaths of the world soon descended into madness.

Unable to fight this threat themselves, the illithids saught help from their former enemies, and together the illithids, duergar, drow, and urdinnir fought back the invaders. Eventually Y'chak seemed to turn his attention elsewhere, and most of the Far Realm aberrations were slain.

5. Peace of Dunspeirrin (139–332 P.A.)

Following the war, the illithids made peace with their former slave races. In the Treaty of Dunspeirrin (139 P.A.), the illithids granted their former slaves control of the entire Middleworld, while retaining for themselves the Lowerworld.

This treaty led to a long period of relative peace, broken only by occasional periods of unrest:

  • In 163 P.A., the goblin cleric Gashkag united a large number of goblin, bugbear, orc, ogre, and troll tribes under his command, and began a war against the dwarven and elven civilizations. This war lasted for seven years, but eventually the goblins were crushed.
  • In 238 P.A., the drow queen Chandara united all of the drow houses, and began a war of genocide against the duergar and urdinnir. The drow captured several major dwarf cities, but the war ended unexpectedly when Chandara was assassinated by the head of a rival house.
  • In 311 P.A., the Great Horde of gibberlings swept through the world, destroying everything in their wake. This menace fell most heavily upon the urdinnir, who lost many of their greatest cities to the unstoppable hordes. The origin of the gibberlings remains a mystery, though sages have speculated that they may be extraplanar in origin, or perhaps they may have been bred by the illithids.

6. Devastation War (333–345 P.A.)

In 333 P.A., a party of adventurers discovered that the illithids were in the process of reconstructing the thoughtstone, and intended to use it to regain mental control of all inhabitants of the world.

Shortly after this plot was exposed, the urdinnir completely disappeared from the world.  Caverns containing their largest remaining cities were destroyed in great cave‑ins, while smaller dwarf settlements were simply abandoned.  Though the illithids were initially blamed, it was later revealed that the urdinnir had planned their disappearance, and had left this world for some undisclosed extraplanar location.

The remaining races immediately declared war against the illithids, and began advancing their armies into the Lowerworld. What they found there surprised them:

  1. Illithid cities had grown considerably, and the illithid civilization had spread throughout the Lowerworld. In addition, several portals were discovered to domed illithid cities that had somehow been constructed on the surface.
  2. The illithids had enslaved a new race that they had found in the Lowerworld (now known as the grimlocks) and had bred them mercilessly. The grimlocks proved quite fierce in battle, and provided much more resistance than was expected to the advancing armies of dwarves and elves.

In addition to their armies of grimlocks, the illithids had another weapon in their arsenal of enormous power, which they referred to as the “Vatholaunt”. This weapon had been carefully constructed by the illithids over the course of a century, and was the size of a small city. It had two main effects:

  1. When activated, the Vatholaunt created a series of directed earthquakes of terrible power. Over the course of a few weeks, the illithids used these earthquakes to destroy most of the major drow and duergar cities in the Middleworld.
  2. The weapon used an enormous amount of magical energy, which it drained from the rest of the world. Following the first use of the weapon, most magic items in the world temporarily ceased to function, arcane and divine casters became entirely unable to cast spells, and divine casters' prayers went unanswered.

A few weeks into the war, most major dwarf and elf cities had been leveled, the gnomes had been removed from the battlefield (with many of their armies sealed into their inaccessible cities), and drow magic had been rendered useless.

Fortunately, the psionic powers of the dwarves were unaffected by the loss of magic, and dwarf psions were successfully able to locate the Vatholaunt.  After a series of desperate military campaigns against grimlock armies, one dwarven division managed to break through to the Lowerworld and destroy the device.

Following this success, the dwarves and elves waged a series of defensive campaigns against the advancing armies of grimlocks. Over the course of a year, the illithids managed to capture several major cities, including Dunspeirrin, Menzoberranzan, Undrek'Thoz, Eryndlyn, and Gracklstugh.

Roughly fourteen months after the war began, magic slowly started to work again, with full magic being restored after another month or so. Suddenly gnome armies became available, and the drow armies regained their substantial might.  Using this advantage, the dwarves and elves managed to retake most of their greatest cities in a series of campaigns over the course of twelve years, though a few still remain in the hands of the illithids.

7. Recent History (345–461 P.A.)

At the end of the Devastation War, a drow lich cleric named Mi'zri siezed control of the drow armies, and launched a surprise attack against the dwarves. This led to a century-long series of wars between the dwarves and the elves, which have severely weakened both civilizations. Some important events during this time include:

  • In 372 P.A., the chitines (a race of spider people who had been slaves of the drow) revolted, sacking several drow cities and severely hobbling the drow military effort.  The lich Mi'zri was killed during this revolt.
  • In 404 P.A., another horde of gibberlings swept through the world.  Though not as terrible as the Great Horde, they did considerable damage, with the destruction falling most heavily on the duergar.
  • In 428 P.A., for reasons that have still not been revealed, the demon goddess Lolth worshipped by the drow suddenly stopped answering prayers. The silence of Lolth lasted for approximately one year, and led to major uphevals in drow society. Seizing upon this opportunity, the dwarves launched a major attack against Menzoberranzan, leading to a decade of war.
  • In 442 P.A., drow worshippers of the mysterious “Elder Elemental Eye” seized control of several major drow cities, and sent armies to conquer both the duergar and the rest of drow civilization. These armies were led by terrible creatures summoned from the Far Realm, which has led some sages to speculate that the Elder Elemental Eye must somehow be connected with one of the Elder Evils. This drow civil war lasted for seven years, but the worshippers of elemental evil were finally defeated by the armies of Lolth.
  • In 451 P.A., the drow discovered the location of the gnome city of Blingdenstone and promptly destroyed it. If anything, this has increased the gnomes' paranoia, and gnome travelers and merchants have become rarer and rarer over the last decade.

Throughout these wars, the illithids have been largely silent, with the exception of the occasional raid for slaves or food. Presumably they have been rebuilding their forces, and perhaps constructing some other terrible weapon. With the dwarves, elves, and gnomes at each other's throats, the time seems ripe for an illithid conquest of the Middleworld.
